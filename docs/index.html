AISTATS 2023 paper project page

Using Sliced Mutual Information to Study Memorization and Generalization in Deep Neural Networks

A practical information-theoretic lens for tracking what deep neural networks preserve, discard, generalize, and memorize across layers and training time.

Read the paper Run the code Copy citation

Authors

Shelvia Wongso, Rohan Ghosh, Mehul Motani

Venue

AISTATS 2023

Focus

Memorization, generalization, label noise, and layer-wise representation analysis

What the paper asks

Neural networks can learn robust patterns, but they can also fit corrupted labels and spurious structure. Accuracy tells us whether the model succeeds; Sliced Mutual Information helps inspect what its internal representations are carrying along the way.

1

How does information move through layers?

SMI tracks how much label-relevant information appears in hidden representations at different depths.

2

When does memorization emerge?

By comparing clean and noisy-label regimes, SMI helps expose when models begin encoding corrupted labels.

3

Can SMI predict generalization?

The experiments study whether SMI is informative for generalization-gap behavior across model settings.

Why sliced mutual information?

Direct mutual-information estimation is difficult for high-dimensional neural representations. SMI makes the problem more tractable by repeatedly projecting representations onto random low-dimensional directions, estimating mutual information in those projected spaces, and averaging the estimates.

Estimator sketch

sample random projections project hidden representations estimate MI per projection average projection estimates SMI(X, Y) ~= mean_theta MI(theta_X^T X, theta_Y^T Y)

Results at a glance

The experiments use SMI as a diagnostic for representation learning, memorization, and generalization.

  • Layer-wise SMI reveals training dynamics: hidden layers show changing SMI patterns across epochs, making representation evolution visible beyond final accuracy.
  • Label-noise experiments expose memorization: comparing clean and corrupted labels helps identify when networks encode noisy supervision rather than generalizable structure.
  • SMI supports generalization analysis: the paper studies SMI alongside generalization gap, regularization, and architecture choices to characterize when learned representations transfer to test data.

Code and reproducibility

This repository contains SMI estimators, KSG and neural MI baselines, experiment runners, aggregation scripts, plotting utilities, and notebooks for interactive inspection.
